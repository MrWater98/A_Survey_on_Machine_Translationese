\begin{abstract}
  The field of machine translation (MT) has evolved rapidly, moving from statistical (SMT) and neural (NMT) models to the current dominance of Large Language Models (LLMs). A constant side effect is ``translationese''---patterns that separate machine translations from human writing. These traits have changed alongside MT systems, shifting from the obvious grammar mistakes of early models to the more subtle biases like simplification and over-literalism seen in NMT and LLM outputs today. This paper surveys the field, tracking how methods for detecting and managing translationese have progressed. 
  %Finally, we highlight ongoing challenges and future directions for managing this phenomenon in an era of ubiquitous MT.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The field of machine translation (MT) has changed dramatically, moving from rule-based systems to today's Large Language Models (LLMs). This progression, marked by milestones like phrase-based Statistical MT (SMT) \cite{koehn2003statistical}, the attention mechanism in Neural MT (NMT) \cite{bahdanau2015neural}, and the Transformer architecture \cite{vaswani2017attention}, has improved translation quality and access. But a constant side effect is ``translationese''---linguistic patterns that separate translated text from the target language's original writing \cite{gellerstam1986translationese}. Understanding how translationese evolves isn't just an academic exercise; it's essential for improving MT systems, ensuring fairness, and preventing long-term risks like training data degradation.

Translationese isn't one single thing; its traits have changed alongside MT paradigms. Early corpus studies, based on concepts like translation universals \cite{baker1993corpus}, found general features like simplification and normalization in human translations \cite{laviosa1998corpus}. The SMT era focused on source-language interference, with researchers showing that ``source language markers'' could identify a translation's original language with high accuracy \cite{koppel2011translationese,van2008source}. This showed that even optimized systems carry traces of their source.

The advent of NMT, while reducing overt grammatical errors, introduced more subtle biases. These include a continued loss of lexical richness \cite{vanmassenhove2019getting}, a tendency towards more conservative and less creative language use, and the emergence of ``post-editese,'' a hybrid artifact found in human-edited NMT outputs that retains some machine-like qualities \cite{toral2019post}. Most recently, LLMs have been shown to produce their own distinct form of translationese. While capable of highly fluent output, they often exhibit excessive literalism, a phenomenon where the model adheres too closely to the source text's structure, and a strong tendency to ``over-normalize'' text, smoothing out stylistic variations \cite{li2025lost,wang2024benchmarking}. These subtle artifacts can be difficult for human evaluators to detect but can have significant downstream effects.



In response to this evolving challenge, a substantial body of research has developed methods to monitor, document, and mitigate the effects of translationese. The task of detecting translationese has progressed from manual analysis and feature-based classifiers, which relied on hand-crafted linguistic features \cite{baroni2006new,ilisei2010identifying,volansky2015features}, to highly accurate neural models that can learn relevant features automatically \citet{pylypenko2021comparing} and \citet{amponsah2022explaining}. Characterizing the specific features of translationese across different MT architectures has also been a key focus, with studies comparing the outputs of SMT, NMT, and LLM-based systems to pinpoint their unique linguistic fingerprints \cite{riley2020translationese}. Furthermore, various mitigation strategies have been proposed. These range from data-centric approaches, such as filtering training data to remove translationese \cite{lembersky2013improving}, to model-centric techniques, like fine-tuning models to better emulate human-written text \cite{jourdan2025translationese}. Some work has even explored leveraging translationese as a form of synthetic data to aid low-resource languages \cite{dabre2024pretraining}. The proliferation of machine-generated text also raises long-term concerns, such as ``model collapse,'' where models trained on synthetic data experience a decline in performance over time \cite{shumailov2024ai}.



To provide a clear and structured overview of this complex, co-evolving landscape, this survey adopts a four-pillar framework. Figure~\ref{fig:mt-timeline} illustrates the co-evolution of machine translation technologies and translationese characteristics over three decades, categorized into four eras: (1) the \textit{Corpus-Based Era} (1990s--2000s) which established foundational concepts like simplification and normalization \cite{baker1993corpus,laviosa1998corpus}; (2) the \textit{SMT Era} (2003--2015), where awkward literalism and source interference were identified through hand-crafted linguistic features \cite{baroni2006new,koehn2003statistical}; (3) the \textit{NMT Era} (2015--2020), marked by deceptive fluency and the loss of lexical diversity, shifting detection towards neural feature learning \cite{bahdanau2015neural,pylypenko2021comparing,vaswani2017attention}; and (4) the current \textit{LLM Era} (2020--present), where excessive literalism and over-normalization pose new challenges for detection through prompt-based evaluation \cite{li2025lost,wang2024benchmarking}. Critically, the figure also highlights the emerging risk of \textit{model collapse} due to recursive training on synthetic data \cite{shumailov2024ai}.



Based on this historical overview, this survey is structured as follows. We first establish a unified taxonomy in Section 2, defining the core research areas. Following this, we dedicate separate sections to each pillar. Section 3, \textit{Identifying Translationese}, reviews the progression of methods for characterizing and detecting translationese artifacts. Section 4, \textit{Evaluating its Impact}, surveys the effects of translationese on translation quality and algorithmic fairness. Section 5, \textit{Managing its Effects}, details the development of strategies to both mitigate and leverage translationese. Finally, Section 6 discusses \textit{Future Directions and Open Problems}, including the long-term risks of model collapse. This structure allows readers to either gain a deep understanding of a specific research pillar or to trace the historical development across all areas, providing a comprehensive and accessible guide for both newcomers and experienced researchers.

\section{Background: The Evolving Nature of Machine Translationese}

Translationese refers to the distinctive linguistic characteristics that make translated text identifiably different from text originally written in the target language \cite{gellerstam1986translationese}. Initially studied in the context of human translation, these artifacts, or ``translation universals,'' include phenomena such as simplification (the tendency to use simpler language), normalization (the tendency to exaggerate features of the target language), and interference from the source language \cite{baker1993corpus}. As machine translation (MT) has evolved from rule-based systems to the sophisticated Large Language Models (LLMs) of today, the nature of machine translationese has co-evolved, shifting from overt grammatical errors to more subtle, systemic biases. To provide an initial insight into this evolution, we present classic examples of machine translationese from three distinct technological eras.

\subsection{The SMT Era: Awkward Literalism}

Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) systems, which dominated the field in the 2000s, operated by learning statistical alignments between phrases in a source and target language. While a significant leap forward, this phrase-based approach often resulted in outputs that were locally fluent but globally disfluent and awkward. The translationese of this era is characterized by literal, word-for-word translations of idioms and unnatural word order, making it easily noticeable.

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Source (Chinese):} \textit{他画蛇添足，结果把事情搞砸了。}

\textbf{Hypothetical SMT Output:} \textit{He drew a snake and added feet, and as a result messed up the matter.}

\textbf{Human Translation:} \textit{He ruined the matter by adding something superfluous.}
\end{quote}

In this example, the SMT system literally translates the idiom \textit{画蛇添足} (huà shé tiān zú), failing to capture its actual meaning of ruining something by doing more than is necessary. The output is a grammatically awkward and nonsensical sentence that clearly exposes the system's lack of deeper semantic understanding.

\subsection{The NMT Era: Deceptive Fluency}

Neural Machine Translation (NMT), particularly with the advent of the Transformer architecture, revolutionized the field by producing highly fluent and grammatically correct translations. However, this fluency can be deceptive. The translationese of the NMT era became more subtle, manifesting as a loss of lexical diversity, simplification of complex syntax, and a general tendency towards ``safe'' or generic language. As \citet{riley2020translationese} demonstrate, NMT models often produce outputs that achieve high automatic scores (like BLEU) but are disliked by human raters because they feel unnatural. The output is correct, but it often feels bland and lacks the richness of the source text.

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Source (German):} \textit{Die Gesetzgebung, ein labyrinthisches Geflecht von Klauseln und Unterklauseln, das über Monate kontroverser Debatten zusammengewoben wurde, wurde schließlich verabschiedet, wenn auch mit einem spürbaren Gefühl der müden Resignation aller beteiligten Parteien.}

\textbf{Hypothetical NMT Output:} \textit{The complex law was finally passed after a long debate, but all parties felt tired and resigned.}

\textbf{Human Translation:} \textit{The legislation, a labyrinthine tapestry of clauses and sub-clauses woven together over months of contentious debate, finally passed, albeit with a palpable sense of weary resignation from all parties involved.}
\end{quote}

Here, the NMT output is perfectly readable but has stripped the source text of its evocative vocabulary (``labyrinthisches Geflecht'' translated as ``complex law'' instead of ``labyrinthine tapestry'') and flattened its complex sentence structure. This simplification and normalization make the text less engaging and lose the stylistic nuance of the original author.

\subsection{The LLM Era: Subtle Over-Adherence}

Large Language Models (LLMs) represent the current state-of-the-art, capable of producing translations that are often indistinguishable from human-written text. The translationese of this era is even more insidious. It is characterized by excessive literalism, where the model adheres too closely to the source text's syntactic structure even when a more natural target-language construction exists, and over-normalization, where it smooths out unique stylistic choices in favor of highly probable, generic phrasing. \citet{li2025lost} systematically evaluate this phenomenon, showing that even powerful LLMs produce ``unexpected unnatural translations'' due to biases introduced during supervised fine-tuning.

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Source (Chinese):} \textit{少样本LLMs仍然落后于常规微调。}

\textbf{LLM Output (from \citet{li2025lost}):} \textit{Few-shot LLMs still lag behind vanilla fine-tuned models in the task.}

\textbf{Refined Human Translation:} \textit{Few-shot LLMs still lag behind vanilla fine-tuning.}
\end{quote}

In this example, cited directly from \citet{li2025lost}, the LLM output is overly literal. It translates ``常规微调'' (chángguī wēitiáo) as ``vanilla fine-tuned models in the task,'' which is a wordy and unnatural phrasing in English. The refined version, ``vanilla fine-tuning,'' is more concise and natural. This demonstrates the challenge of LLM-era translationese: it is not about what is wrong, but about what is lost in the subtle choices of phrasing and style.

These examples illustrate that as MT systems have become more powerful, machine translationese has not disappeared but has merely become more difficult to detect. Understanding this evolution is crucial for developing robust methods for identifying, evaluating, and managing its effects, a challenge that this survey aims to address.

\section{Tracking and Spotting Translationese}

This section tracks how researchers have tried to pin down what translationese actually looks like and how to catch it. We've moved from looking at physical books in the 90s to using neural networks that learn patterns on their own today.

\subsection{Lessons from Corpus Studies}

Systematic study of translationese started in the 1990s with Corpus-Based Translation Studies (CTS). Before that, people mostly just told stories about "weird" translations in literature. CTS changed things by letting researchers compare huge piles of translated and original text to find real, measurable differences \cite{laviosa1998corpus}. This work led to a few big ideas, often called ``translation universals.''

One big idea is \textbf{simplification}: translated text is often easier to read than the original. This shows up as a smaller range of words (lower type-token ratio), shorter sentences, and a preference for common grammar \cite{baker1993corpus}. It’s as if translators are trying to lower the mental load for the reader.

Then there's \textbf{explicitation}. Translators tend to spell things out that the original author left implicit. They might add "however" or "therefore" to make the logic clearer, or explain a cultural reference that wouldn't make sense in the new language. It makes things clear, but also a bit wordy.

Finally, \textbf{normalization} (or conventionalization) is the habit of making translated text sound "too" normal. Translators often avoid creative or weird language, sticking instead to safe, standard phrases. As \citet{gellerstam1986translationese} noted in his study of Swedish novels, translated text often feels "flatter" than original writing.

Not everyone agrees these are "laws." Some argue they depend on the languages involved or the translator's skill \cite{zanettin2013corpus}. There's also \textbf{interference} (or ``shining-through''), where the structure of the source language just leaks into the target. Regardless of the debate, these concepts gave us the first real toolkit for studying translationese.

\subsection{The SMT Era: Hunting for Features}

The rise of Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in the 2000s gave us mountains of machine-generated text to analyze. The goal shifted from just describing translationese to actually predicting it. We started treating it as a game of "Spot the Translation."

The standard move was to use Support Vector Machines (SVMs) to draw a line between original and translated text. The real work wasn't the math, though—it was picking the right "features" to feed the model. 

Researchers looked at:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lexical Features:} Word counts, n-gram frequencies, and how often "stop words" (like "the" or "and") appeared.
    \item \textbf{Syntactic Features:} Grammar patterns, how deep the sentence trees were, and how often things like the passive voice showed up.
    \item \textbf{Information-Theoretic Features:} Measuring how "predictable" a text was. Since translationese is often more conventional, it's usually less surprising to a language model.
\end{itemize}

These feature-heavy models were surprisingly good, often hitting over 90\% accuracy \cite{baroni2006new, ilisei2010identifying}. We also learned that translationese has "dialects" that change depending on the source language \cite{koppel2011translationese}. This wasn't just for fun; knowing these patterns helped researchers build better SMT systems \cite{lembersky2013improving}.

\subsection{Neural Models: Learning the Patterns}

When Neural Machine Translation (NMT) took over in the mid-2010s, things got trickier. NMT output (especially from Transformers \cite{vaswani2017attention}) sounds much more natural than old SMT output. The obvious mistakes were gone, replaced by subtle biases like a lack of word variety or "safe" phrasing \cite{vanmassenhove2019getting}.

We also stopped picking features by hand. Instead, we started using models like BERT to learn the features directly from the data. \citet{pylypenko2021comparing} found that while these neural models work better, they're also "black boxes." This led to a new focus on \textbf{explainability}—trying to see if the models were actually learning the same grammar rules we used to code by hand \cite{amponsah2022explaining}.

This era also gave us "post-editese" \cite{toral2019post}—the weird hybrid language that happens when humans edit NMT output. Some researchers even started thinking of translationese as its own distinct language with its own internal rules \cite{riley2020translationese}.

\subsection{The LLM Era: A New Set of Problems}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have changed the game again. They're so good at writing that old-school detection tricks often fail. The problem now isn't that the translation is wrong, but that it's "too" literal or "too" smooth \cite{li2025lost,wang2024benchmarking}.

Since we can't always rely on simple classifiers anymore, we're using the LLMs themselves as detectors. We write prompts asking them to rate how "natural" or "idiomatic" a sentence is \cite{dabre2024pretraining}. We're also moving toward more detailed style analysis, like "literalism scores" that measure how much the model is just copying the source structure \cite{li2025lost}.

We're also finding that every model has its own "fingerprint." A model trained mostly on human text might write differently than one trained on synthetic data \cite{wang2024benchmarking}. This is especially important for low-resource languages, where translationese can actually make existing biases worse \cite{jourdan2025translationese}. The challenge now is building tools that can spot these subtle artifacts in real-time without losing the benefits of the models.

\section{Why Translationese Matters: Quality, Bias, and Fairness}

Translationese isn't just an academic curiosity; it has real-world consequences for how we build and evaluate MT systems. These linguistic artifacts distort quality metrics, bake in social biases, and threaten the diversity of digital text. We look at these impacts through three main areas: the "human parity" debate, algorithmic homogenization, and demographic representation.

\subsection{Quality Metrics and the Human Parity Debate}

The link between translationese and translation quality is messy. While human readers usually find unnatural text to be lower quality, automatic metrics often see things differently. This gap has created a lot of confusion, especially around the idea of ``human parity.''

The biggest issue is that translationese can actually inflate scores like BLEU. \citet{riley2020translationese} showed that when an NMT model is pushed to produce more translationese, its BLEU scores go up, even though human evaluators say the output is worse. This happens because translationese uses simpler, more predictable words. Since the "reference" translations used for testing are often translations themselves, a model that mimics translationese is actually better at matching those references. 

This is why claims that AI has reached "human parity" are so controversial. Several studies claimed AI was as good as professional translators, but later research found that the tests were flawed. \citet{toral2018reassessing} and \citet{freitag2019translationese} pointed out that these tests often used news articles already translated from another language. If you're testing an AI on its ability to copy human translationese, you're not really testing if it can write like a native. \citet{freitag2019translationese} recommends throwing out this kind of "reverse-created" test data entirely.

There's also a math problem. \citet{freitag2019translationese} noted that many parity studies use tests with low statistical power. This means they might fail to find a difference between humans and machines simply because the sample size is too small, leading to an unsubstantiated claim of parity. 

In short, translationese in our data makes our metrics unreliable. To reach true human-level quality, we need systems that don't just match a reference, but write text free of the machine artifacts that give them away.

\subsection{Algorithmic Bias and Homogenization}

Translationese also poses a long-term risk: it makes our language less diverse. This is often called \textbf{homogenization}. Because MT models learn from statistical averages, they tend to produce text that is safe, conventional, and boring. They smooth over the unique styles and creative touches that make human writing interesting. \citet{sizov2024analysing} found that even advanced LLMs are less stylistically adventurous than human translators.

This creates a dangerous feedback loop. As more machine-generated text is posted online, future models are trained on that same boring text. This can lead to \textbf{model collapse} \cite{shumailov2024ai}. When models are trained recursively on their own output, they start to forget the rare words and complex structures found in the "tails" of human language. Eventually, the model can only produce generic, bland text.

This isn't just a theory anymore. With so much LLM content on the web, new models are inevitably being trained on synthetic data. This could eventually stop MT systems from getting any better. Filtering out translationese isn't just about making one translation better; it's about keeping our digital language healthy.

\subsection{Gender and Demographic Bias}

The flattening effect of translationese also affects how people are represented. In their drive to be "normal," models often default to the most common stereotypes, which shows up clearly in gender representation.

Gender bias is an old problem in MT. When translating from a language like English (which has little grammatical gender) to French or Spanish, the model often has to pick a gender for words like "doctor" or "nurse." Models trained on biased data usually default to masculine forms \cite{vanmassenhove2019getting}. Translationese makes this worse because its push for normalization reinforces these statistical averages.

This is a major hurdle for inclusive language. Translationese is conservative by nature, which clashes with the new ways people use language to represent non-binary identities. \citet{jourdan2025fairtranslate} tested leading LLMs on their FairTranslate dataset and found significant biases. The models often failed to correctly use the singular `they' or neopronouns, choosing instead to "correct" them back to traditional binary forms.

This happens because models optimize for what is statistically likely, not what is socially fair. Since inclusive language is less common in old training data, a model guided by translationese sees it as "noise" to be smoothed over. This doesn't just hurt the style; it makes certain groups of people invisible. 

The same problem affects cultural honorifics and local dialects. Everything gets flattened into a statistically dominant worldview. For low-resource cultures, this effect is even more damaging, as it erases their unique linguistic traits in favor of a narrow, global average.

\section{How to Fix (and Use) Translationese}

Researchers are no longer just spotting translationese; they're trying to manage it. This section looks at how we can reduce its negative effects—like quality loss and bias—and even how to use its unique traits for good.

\subsection{Cleaning the Data}

Since MT models are basically mirrors of their training data, most fixes start there.

\textbf{Filtering and Weighting:} One simple fix is to just remove translated text from training sets. \citet{lembersky2013improving} showed back in the SMT era that models trained only on original, natively written text performed better than those trained on translationese, even when the translationese sets were much larger. Today, we use more advanced classifiers to filter out "low-quality" synthetic data or prioritize human writing \cite{pylypenko2021comparing}.

\textbf{Managing Synthetic Data:} Back-translation is a standard tool for improving fluency, but it introduces its own artifacts. \citet{riley2020translationese} suggested using tags to tell the model which data is authentic and which is synthetic. This stops the model from accidentally learning and repeating the quirks of machine-generated text.

\textbf{Structural Restoration:} Some try to "undo" the simplification that happens during translation. \citet{chen2012simplification} proposed a framework that first simplifies text to make it easier to translate, then "restores" the original style and complexity afterward. It's a way to fight the natural flattening effect of MT.

\subsection{Building Better Models}

We can also change how the models themselves work.

\textbf{Fine-tuning:} How we fine-tune models matters. \citet{li2025lost} argue that training LLMs on high-quality human translations can actually backfire by making the models too literal. They suggest using training goals that reward natural style instead of just matching the source text. Others are looking at fine-tuning to stop models from erasing inclusive language \cite{jourdan2025translationese}.

\textbf{Prompting and ICL:} For LLMs, we can often fix translationese just by how we ask. By giving the model examples of natural human writing or explicitly telling it to avoid being too "stiff," we can improve the output without any extra training \cite{wang2024benchmarking}. 

\textbf{Inference Tricks:} We can also tweak the model during the actual translation. Sampling techniques like nucleus sampling can be tuned to pick less predictable, more interesting words. We can even build "translationese-aware" metrics directly into the process to guide the model toward more human-sounding results.

\subsection{Using Translationese for Good}

Translationese isn't always a bad thing. It has unique properties we can actually use.

\textbf{Low-Resource Help:} When we don't have much human-translated data, machine-generated translationese can be a life-saver. \citet{dabre2024pretraining} found that pre-training on large amounts of "messy" machine text gives a solid foundation for low-resource languages, which can then be polished with a bit of human data.

\textbf{Style Selection:} \citet{riley2020translationese} treat translationese as just another "style." By tagging it, a model can learn to translate \textit{into} or \textit{away from} that style, letting users choose how natural or literal they want the result to be.

\textbf{Spotting Bad Translations:} The very markers that define translationese are great for quality control. If a translation is too simplified or too literal, we can flag it for a human to check \cite{toral2018reassessing, sizov2024analysing}.

\section{Where We Go From Here}

Even after decades of work, translationese is still changing. As we move toward massive generative models, new problems are popping up that need more than just a math-based fix.

\subsection{Escaping the Feedback Loop}

As the internet fills up with machine-generated text, the risk of \textbf{model collapse} is real \cite{shumailov2024ai}. Spotting translationese isn't just a stylistic choice anymore—it's necessary to keep our training data clean. Future research should focus on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Digital Watermarking:} Adding hidden signals to MT output so we can easily find it later.
    \item \textbf{Better Classifiers:} Building systems that can reliably tell the difference between human translationese and machine artifacts. This means looking beyond simple word counts toward deeper measures like entropy to see how "predictable" the text is \cite{wang2024benchmarking}.
    \item \textbf{Data Recovery:} Finding ways to "un-flatten" synthetic text to bring back the original richness of human language.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Translationese in New Settings}

We still don't fully understand how translationese affects \textbf{zero-shot and low-resource learning}. While it can be a useful starting point \cite{dabre2024pretraining, riley2020translationese}, it also risks baking in permanent biases in languages where we don't have much human data. We need to know if translationese is a "universal bridge" that helps models learn, or a "ceiling" that keeps them from ever sounding truly native. 

\subsection{Humans in the Loop}

The line between human and machine writing is blurring. When humans edit MT output, they produce \textbf{post-editese} \cite{toral2019post}. We need to know:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mental Load:} Does reading machine translationese "prime" humans to write more like machines?
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Help:} Can we build systems that spot translationese as you type and suggest more natural alternatives?
    \item \textbf{Bigger Context:} How do we handle translationese in long documents or in videos where the flow of conversation matters?
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

This survey has tracked how translationese research moved from describing literary quirks to solving core problems in Large Language Models. What used to be a set of interesting "universals"—like simplification and normalization—is now a major factor in whether our AI is fair, high-quality, and sustainable.

Modern neural models are incredibly fluent, but they haven't "solved" translationese. Instead, they've made it harder to see. These subtle machine fingerprints still mess with our metrics, erase diversity, and threaten the future of the training data we rely on.

But translationese isn't just a bug. It's a lens. It helps us see how our models think and what makes human translation unique. By understanding it, we can build MT systems that are more flexible, more helpful for low-resource languages, and better at assessing their own quality.

Going forward, the goal isn't just to match the statistics of a reference translation. It's to achieve \textbf{linguistic authenticity}. We need models that are aware of style, sensitive to culture, and capable of preserving the messiness and richness of human speech.
