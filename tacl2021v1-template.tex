\section{Introduction}

% [Polish] Original: The field of machine translation (MT) has undergone a dramatic evolution, from rule-based systems to the sophisticated Large Language Models (LLMs) of today. This progression, marked by milestones such as phrase-based Statistical MT (SMT) \cite{koehn2003statistical}, the attention mechanism in Neural MT (NMT) \cite{bahdanau2015neural}, and the Transformer architecture \cite{vaswani2017attention}, has significantly enhanced translation quality and accessibility. However, a persistent artifact of this technological advancement is ``translationese''---systematic linguistic patterns that distinguish translated text from text originally authored in the target language \cite{gellerstam1986translationese}. Understanding the nature and evolution of translationese is not merely an academic exercise; it is critical for improving MT systems, ensuring fairness, and mitigating long-term risks, such as the degradation of training data.
Machine translation (MT) has evolved dramatically from phrase-based Statistical MT (SMT) \cite{koehn2003statistical} and Neural MT (NMT) \cite{vaswani2017attention} to Large Language Models (LLMs). A persistent by-product of this advancement is ``translationese''---systematic linguistic patterns distinguishing translated text from text originally authored in the target language \cite{gellerstam1986translationese}. Understanding translationese is critical for improving MT systems, ensuring fairness, and mitigating long-term risks like training data degradation.

Translationese characteristics have co-evolved with MT paradigms. Figure~\ref{fig:mt-timeline} illustrates this co-evolution over three decades across four eras.

The \textit{Corpus-Based Era} (1990s--2000s) laid the empirical groundwork, identifying translation universals like simplification, normalization, and explicitation \cite{baker1993corpus,laviosa1998corpus}. The \textit{SMT Era} (2000s) shifted focus to predictive modeling, utilizing feature-based classifiers for binary detection and identifying source-language interference \cite{baroni2006new,volansky2015features,koppel2011translationese}. The \textit{NMT Era} (mid-2010s) brought fluency but introduced subtle biases like loss of lexical richness and stylistic flattening, alongside the emergence of ``post-editese'' \cite{vanmassenhove2018getting,toral2019post}. Detection shifted to neural feature-learning \cite{pylypenko2021comparing}. Finally, the \textit{LLM Era} presents near-human fluency but distinct translationese characterized by excessive literalism and over-normalization \cite{wang2024benchmarking}. This pervasive issue in multilingual resources \cite{church2025emerging} raises existential risks like model collapse \cite{shumailov2024ai}, making detection an urgent challenge \cite{li2025lost}.

To provide a structured overview of this complex landscape, this survey adopts a four-pillar framework: (1) \textit{Identifying Translationese}, reviewing detection methods; (2) \textit{Evaluating its Impact}, surveying effects on quality and fairness; (3) \textit{Managing its Effects}, detailing mitigation and leverage strategies; and (4) \textit{Future Directions}, discussing open problems like model collapse.

\subsection{Foundational Concepts from Corpus-Based Studies}

% [Polish] Original: The systematic study of translationese originates from the field of Corpus-Based Translation Studies (CTS), which emerged in the 1990s. The systematic study of translationese originates from the field of Corpus-Based Translation Studies (CTS), which emerged in the 1990s. Before this, observations about the peculiar nature of translated text were largely anecdotal and confined to literary criticism. CTS provided a new paradigm, enabling researchers to analyze large electronic corpora of translated and non-translated texts to identify systematic, quantifiable differences \cite{laviosa1998corpus}. This empirical approach led to the formulation of several influential hypotheses about the nature of translation, often referred to as ``translation universals.''

\subsection{Feature-Based Methods in the SMT Era}

The rise of Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in the 2000s marked a pivotal moment for translationese research. For the first time, large quantities of machine-generated text were available for analysis, and the task of identifying translationese shifted from a descriptive, linguistic endeavor to a predictive, computational one. The central goal became to automatically distinguish between original text and translated text, a task that was formalized as a binary classification problem.

\subsection{Neural Approaches in the NMT Era}

The transition to NMT in the mid-2010s brought another paradigm shift. NMT models, especially Transformers \cite{vaswani2017attention}, produced highly fluent translations, leading to two major consequences. First, translationese became subtler. Overt grammatical errors vanished, replaced by nuanced biases like lost lexical diversity, conservative language use, and stylistic flattening \cite{vanmassenhove2018getting}. Second, methodology shifted from \textbf{feature engineering} to \textbf{feature learning}. Researchers used deep neural networks like BERT to learn features directly. \citet{pylypenko2021comparing} found that while neural models performed better, combining both approaches yielded the best results.

% [Polish] Original: This shift also brought a new focus on \textbf{explainability}. Since neural models are often black boxes, researchers sought to understand \textit{what} features the models were learning to distinguish between original and translated text. Techniques like attention visualization and probing tasks were used to interpret the models' decisions. For example, \citet{amponsah2022explaining} used attention mechanisms to show that neural models were implicitly learning some of the same syntactic and lexical features that had been hand-crafted in the SMT era.
This shift brought a focus on \textbf{explainability}. Researchers used attention visualization and probing tasks to understand what neural ``black boxes'' learned, revealing they implicitly captured SMT-era syntactic and lexical features \cite{amponsah2022explaining}. New concepts also emerged: \citet{toral2019post} introduced ``post-editese'' (language from human post-editing of MT) as an ``exacerbated translationese,'' while \citet{riley2020translationese} argued translationese functions as a distinct ``language'' in large multilingual models, impacting training and evaluation.

\subsection{Emerging Patterns and Detection Challenges in the LLM Era}

% [Polish] Original: The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has once again reshaped the landscape of translationese research. While the examples in Section~2 illustrate the \textit{characteristics} of LLM translationese (e.g., excessive literalism), this section focuses on the \textit{methodological challenges and opportunities} for its identification. The near-human fluency of LLM outputs renders traditional feature-based and even some neural classification methods less effective, necessitating a shift in research focus from binary classification to more nuanced, fine-grained stylistic analysis \cite{li2025lost,wang2024benchmarking}.
The advent of LLMs reshaped the landscape. Their near-human fluency renders traditional classification methods less effective, shifting focus to fine-grained stylistic analysis \cite{wang2024benchmarking,dutta2022towards}. \citet{church2025emerging} note translationese's proliferation across multilingual resources (e.g., Wikipedia, FLORES), arguing its presence in ``wrong'' translation directions constitutes a systemic quality problem. Because LLMs lack overt statistical fingerprints, smoothing over irregularities, researchers are exploring new detection avenues.

\section{Conclusion}

This survey has traced the evolution of translationese from a set of linguistic universals into a central challenge for modern MT systems. While neural and LLM-based architectures have transformed its manifestations---from overt grammatical errors to subtle homogenization and demographic bias---they have not eliminated it. Addressing translationese is no longer merely a stylistic concern; it is a prerequisite for building MT systems that are fair, robust, and capable of preserving the full diversity of human expression.
